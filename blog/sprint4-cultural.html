Neuroplasticity and Growth Mindset

Neuroplasticity

Neuroplasticity is the idea that your brain is not a fixed, rigid structure. It changes and flexes and grows as you use it and learn more. This understanding can greatly benefit people, as by understanding this concept you can start to take advantage of it.

The old idea of 'you have what you are born with' is outdated and detrimental. It discourages striving to better oneself. By understanding that you can improve and become smarter, it removes those mental barriers and allows for hope and aspiration.

Keep learning. I play music as a semi-professional hobby. All music teachers encourage regular practice, as playing an instrument is definitely a 'use it or lose it' type deal. If you skip a day of practice, you know about it. If you skip two days of practice, everyone knows about it. Neuroplasticity is the same way. If you don't use your brain, if you don't keep learning and forming those connections, you'll lose your neuroplasticity. There are other methods to help increase neuroplasticity as well - things like mindfulness, meditation, getting rest. As an added bonus, these activities are good for your general health and wellbeing too!

Growth Mindset

Having a 'growth mindset' is the idea that you believe in your potential. As we know from our understanding of neuroplasticity, you have the ability to change, grow and learn new things. Having this mindset can be a huge benefit when approaching a difficult challenge.

Let's say a person attempts to solve a difficult puzzle. They do not have a growth mindset. They believe their abilities to be fixed, that there are certain things they just can't do or will never understand, and that attempting to do so is a waste of time. The second that person starts to struggle with something, chances are they will shut down. They will back away from the challenge because they know they can't do it.

Obviously this particular approach is not a very effective one, and there is no room for personal growth

Say this same person approaches the same problem, but this time instead of having a fixed mindset they have a growth mindset. When they encounter trouble, instead of shutting down, they get interested. They try harder. Maybe they solve the problem, maybe not, but they learn something in the process. They are better off having given it a go. This is a growth mindset.

How does this influence my learning?

Being aware of these two concepts will certainly help in my learning. I understand that the brain is not a rigid structure and that it can change. Because of this I know I don't have to be good at or understand everything. With practice my brain will change and eventually I will understand. I can apply the growth mindset. "I don't get it now, but with practice and effort eventually I will". Nothing ever comes easy in life. To get the best rewards, you've got to try your best.
